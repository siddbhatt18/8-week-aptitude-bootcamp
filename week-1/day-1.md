### **Section A: Understanding HCF (Highest Common Factor)**

#### **Definition & Concept (15 minutes)**

**What is HCF?**
- The largest number that divides two or more numbers completely
- Also called GCD (Greatest Common Divisor)

**Real-world Application:**
- Dividing items into equal groups
- Finding optimal package sizes
- Simplifying fractions

#### **Method 1: Prime Factorization Method**

**Step-by-Step Process:**
1. Find prime factors of each number
2. Identify common prime factors
3. Take lowest power of common factors
4. Multiply them together

**Example 1: Find HCF of 36 and 48**
```
36 = 2¬≤ √ó 3¬≤
48 = 2‚Å¥ √ó 3¬π

Common factors: 2 and 3
Lowest powers: 2¬≤ and 3¬π
HCF = 2¬≤ √ó 3 = 4 √ó 3 = 12
```

#### **Method 2: Division Method (Euclidean Algorithm)**

**Step-by-Step Process:**
1. Divide larger number by smaller
2. Divide divisor by remainder
3. Repeat until remainder = 0
4. Last divisor is HCF

**Example 2: Find HCF of 144 and 180**
```
180 = 144 √ó 1 + 36
144 = 36 √ó 4 + 0

HCF = 36
```

### **Section B: Understanding LCM (Least Common Multiple)**

#### **Definition & Concept (15 minutes)**

**What is LCM?**
- The smallest number divisible by all given numbers
- The least positive integer that is a multiple of all numbers

**Real-world Application:**
- Finding when events occur together
- Scheduling repeated tasks
- Working with fractions

#### **Method 1: Prime Factorization for LCM**

**Step-by-Step Process:**
1. Find prime factors of each number
2. Take highest power of each prime factor
3. Multiply all highest powers together

**Example 3: Find LCM of 12, 15, 20**
```
12 = 2¬≤ √ó 3
15 = 3 √ó 5
20 = 2¬≤ √ó 5

All prime factors: 2, 3, 5
Highest powers: 2¬≤, 3¬π, 5¬π
LCM = 2¬≤ √ó 3 √ó 5 = 4 √ó 3 √ó 5 = 60
```

### **Section C: The HCF-LCM Relationship (15 minutes)**

#### **Golden Formula**
```
For two numbers a and b:
HCF(a,b) √ó LCM(a,b) = a √ó b
```

**Important Note:** This formula works ONLY for two numbers!

**Example 4: Using the relationship**
```

Solution:

1728 = 48x
x = 36
```
